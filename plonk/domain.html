Domain

Plonk needs a domain to encode the circuit on. We choose a domain H such that it is a multiplicative subgroup of the scalar field of our curve. Furthermore, as FFT (used for interpolation) works best in domains of size 2k for some k, we choose H to be a subgroup of order 2k. Since the pasta curves both have scalar fields of prime orders p and q, their multiplicative subgroup is of order p−1 and q−1 respectively (without the zero element). As such, the pasta curves were generated specifically to allow this:

the fields of the Pasta curves that were generated for Halo 2 have q−1 as a multiple of 232, so any power of 2 up to 232 can be used.

(see Lagrange’s theorem for more details)

For each curve, we generate a domain H as the set H=1,ω,ω2,⋯. As we work in a multiplicative subgroup, the polynomial that vanishes in all of these points can be written and efficiently calculated as ZH​(x)=x∣H∣−1.
This is because, by definition, ω∣H∣=1. If x∈H, then x=ωi for some i, and we have:
